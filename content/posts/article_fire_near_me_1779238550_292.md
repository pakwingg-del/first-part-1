**Riverside, CA —** In what analysts are calling the “most statistically improbable data cascade of the decade,” a routine scan of fire alerts in Southern California has revealed a chilling anomaly: every single “fire near me” GPS ping over the past 72 hours has originated from the exact same set of geographic coordinates—the back parking lot of a shuttered Blockbuster Video in Riverside.

“It’s a ghost fire,” said Dr. Lena Zhao, a data glitch specialist. “The thermal satellites see it. The 911 dispatch logs route to it. But when crews arrive? Nothing. No smoke. No heat. Just a perfectly dry, two-foot by two-foot patch of asphalt that has been the epicenter of 47 ‘near me’ alerts since Tuesday.”

The real glitch, however, is in the user metadata. Every single person who received the alert—whether in Burbank, San Diego, or Palm Springs—was logged by the app as being within 0.1 miles of the Blockbuster lot at the exact moment the fire was “detected.” Even users who were asleep in their beds 100 miles away.

“We think the System is trying to tell us something,” Zhao added, her voice flat. “The Blockbuster closed in 2018. There’s no electricity. No people. But the fire keeps burning in the data. It’s a recursive loop. A memory of a disaster that hasn’t happened to a place that doesn’t exist.”

When asked what this means for public safety, the only response from the county fire chief was a cryptic nod and a single typed word: "*Rewind.*"
