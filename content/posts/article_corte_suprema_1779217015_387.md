**HEADLINE: CORTE SUPREMA EMITS LANDMARK RULING ON FEDERAL JURISDICTIONAL BOUNDARIES**

**Washington, D.C. – (ANCHOR)** In a 6-3 decision delivered this morning, the Corte Suprema de Justicia de la Nación, the highest judicial authority, issued a landmark ruling that redefines the scope of federal oversight in matters of interstate commerce and environmental regulation. The decision, stemming from the consolidated case of *Mendoza vs. Comisión Federal de Recursos*, establishes a new legal standard for determining when state-level actions may be preempted by federal statutes.

**What?** The ruling establishes a two-part test requiring federal agencies to demonstrate both a "substantial economic effect" on interstate markets and a "clear, non-ambiguous legislative intent" before preempting state laws. This effectively curtails the federal government's ability to unilaterally nullify state-level environmental and commercial regulations.

**Who?** The case was brought by the State of Mendoza, represented by Attorney General Ricardo Alves, against the Comisión Federal de Recursos (CFR), a federal agency. The majority opinion was authored by Chief Justice Elena Martínez, joined by Justices Vargas, Ochoa, Bellini, Cruz, and Fernández. Justice Eduardo Reyes authored the dissenting opinion, arguing the decision "undermines the principle of uniform national standards."

**When?** The ruling was issued officially at 10:00 AM Eastern Time, with immediate effect. Legal analysts expect lower courts to begin applying the new standard in pending cases within 30 days.

**Where?** The decision applies to all 23 provinces and the autonomous capital district, with particular implications for energy, agriculture, and manufacturing sectors operating in states with environmental regulations that differ from federal standards.
