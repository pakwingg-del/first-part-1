**HEADLINE: 🤯 RED LOBSTER’S TALLAHASSEE CLOSURE EXPOSES THE "CANCEL CULTURE OF COMMERCE"—BIBLICAL PROPORTIONS OF ENTITLEMENT?**

**TALLAHASSEE, FL** — In what moral critics are calling a “canary in the coal mine for societal decay,” the sudden shuttering of Red Lobster’s flagship Tallahassee location has sparked a firestorm of ethical debate far beyond bad cheddar biscuits. Social media exploded after a leaked internal memo revealed the closure wasn’t due to slow sales, but an inability to keep up with **$4,000 a week in "unregistered walkouts"** —groups of young patrons allegedly treating the "Endless Shrimp" promotion as a moral loophole.

“We have raised a generation that sees a corporation not as an employer of 100 local families, but as a villain to be exploited,” fumed Dr. Helena Marsh, a cultural ethicist. “They gorge, they post the ‘hauls’ on TikTok, and they skip the bill, bragging it’s ‘sticking it to the man.’ But the man in this story is a single mother of two who just lost her line-cook job.”

The viral clip shows a young woman filming herself finishing a mountain of shrimp tails and saying, “The Bible says we are owed, not borrowed. The Red Lobster can afford it.” Critics argue this reveals a **deep crisis of communal responsibility**—a "tragedy of the commons" where the pleasure of the few dismantles the livelihood of the many.

“Endless shrimp was a promise, not a right,” Marsh concluded. “When we confuse abundance with entitlement, the whole boat sinks. This is the moral bankruptcy of a society that has forgotten that every lobster tail has a cost—and the poorest pay it first.”
