**Headline: The Gilded Verdict: Is the Corte Suprema Repeating the Supreme Court’s 1895 Mistake?**

**Rio de Janeiro, Brazil** — It’s the historical echo no one saw coming. As the *Corte Suprema* deliberates on an unprecedented economic overhaul, historians are drawing a chilling parallel to the U.S. Supreme Court’s infamous *Pollock v. Farmers’ Loan & Trust Co.* (1895) decision.

Much like the *Corte* today, the 1895 Court faced a watershed moment: the introduction of a progressive income tax. The Justices, in a 5-4 ruling, struck it down, arguing it violated direct tax rules. The result? A 20-year “Gilded Age” of unchecked monopoly, deep inequality, and the rise of the Robber Barons. The decision was so unpopular it was effectively overturned by the 16th Amendment.

Now, history buffs are pointing at the *Corte Suprema’s* current ruling on corporate tax shields and land reform. “It’s the same psychological trap,” says Dr. Elena Vargas, a legal historian at the University of São Paulo. “The Court believes it is protecting constitutional parchment, but it is unknowingly lighting the fuse for a populist backlash—just like in 1895.”
