**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE: The Bullet That Broke the Social Contract**

**San Diego, CA** – In what authorities are calling a “symptom, not a sin,” a mass shooting in a downtown San Diego food hall has sparked a heated ethical debate not about the shooter, but about the moral vacuum that allowed him to exist.

The suspected gunman, 34-year-old former tech middle manager Marcus Thorne, left a manifesto that did not contain political grievances or religious extremism, but rather a 300-page spreadsheet detailing a “debt of human decency” he claimed society owed him. The spreadsheet itemized perceived slights: a declined promotion, a LinkedIn post that received only four likes, and a barista who forgot his oat milk.

Critics argue that the true perpetrator is not Thorne, but the culture that raised him—a culture of transactional relationships, digital validation, and the erosion of communal obligation.

“We have spent a generation optimizing for individual convenience and self-esteem over collective responsibility,” said Dr. Helena Vance, a moral philosopher at Stanford. “We told these people they were special, but we never taught them how to be good. The shooter didn’t just wake up broken; he was meticulously built by a society that rewards rage and punishes quiet resilience.”

Social media has exploded. Hashtags like #EthicalCollapse and #YouOweMeNothing duel with calls for more mental health funding. One viral tweet reads: *“We keep asking ‘How could someone do this?’ But the real question is ‘How could we create a world where this makes sense to someone?’”*

Meanwhile, a GoFundMe for the victims has raised $2 million, while a separate, controversial GoFundMe for the shooter’s “alienated youth defense fund” has raised $200,000, with donors leaving comments like “He said what we’re all thinking.”
