**HISTORY REPEATS? Legal Scholars Draw Eerie Parallels Between Supreme Court’s New Term and the Run-Up to *Marbury v. Madison***  

**Washington, D.C.** — As the Supreme Court prepares to hear a slate of cases that could redefine the balance of executive power, a growing chorus of legal historians is sounding an alarm. They’re comparing the current term to the high-stakes chaos of 1803—the year of *Marbury v. Madison*.  

“We’re seeing the same ingredients: a president pushing the limits of authority, a Congress in open conflict with the judiciary, and a Chief Justice forced to decide whether to defend the Court’s own relevance or risk irrelevance,” says Dr. Eliza Harlow, a constitutional historian at Yale. “In 1803, John Marshall chose judicial review. Today, the Court is facing a similar fork in the road—except this time, the stakes are about whether the Court can enforce its own rulings against a defiant executive branch.”  

Historians point to three “hidden patterns” echoing the pre-Marbury era:  
1. **The Midnight Appointments Crisis** — Today, disputes over “shadow dockets” and emergency petitions mirror the partisan back-and-forth of Adams’s last-minute appointments.  
2. **The Power of the Pen** — Just as Jefferson ignored a subpoena from Chief Justice Marshall, modern presidents have tested the limits of executive privilege.  
3. **Public Patronage vs. Judicial Integrity** — Just as the 1803 Court was attacked by Jeffersonian Republicans for being “Federalist strongholds,” today’s Court faces accusations of political bias that threaten to erode its legitimacy.  

“If the Court loses the ability to enforce its own decisions, we don’t just get a repeat of 1803—we get a constitutional crisis that makes the Nullification Crisis look like a polite disagreement,” warns Harlow.
