**SNIPPET:**
WASHINGTON, DC — As the Biden administration scrambles to plug coverage gaps after 2.1 million Americans lost Affordable Care Act enrollment over the past 90 days, an obscure cohort of economic historians is raising eyebrows with a startling comparison: the 1862 Homestead Act land rush.

“The Homestead Act gave away 270 million acres to people who filed first. Once the ‘free land’ was claimed, thousands were left with nothing—except the debt they incurred getting there,” said Dr. Lena Hartfield of the Smithsonian’s American Enterprise Archive. “Today, a patient who signs up for a $0 premium bronze plan and then gets ‘churned’ off a state Medicaid roll is performing the same tragic settler ritual—settle, stake a claim, then get wiped off the digital map.”

The raw numbers tell the story. In the first half of 2024, administrative “unwinding” errors canceled over 500,000 policies for people who were still eligible. Combined with enhanced subsidy expirations, the ACA is hemorrhaging enrollees at rates not seen since the 2017 individual mandate repeal. But historians argue the real crisis is structural, not regulatory.

“We call it the ‘Land Office Loop,’” Hartfield explained. “In 1850, you’d travel weeks to get your 160 acres. In 2024, you spend 45 minutes on HealthCare.gov and still get terminated if a state server glitches out. The difference? In 1850, if you failed, you could squat. In 2024, the only squatting is in the ER waiting room.”
