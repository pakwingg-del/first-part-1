**BREAKING NEWS ALERT**

**DATE:** 17 October 2023

**LOCATION:** Rome, Italy — Palazzo della Consulta

**SUBJECT:** Supreme Court of Cassation (Corte Suprema di Cassazione)

**WHAT:** The Italian Supreme Court of Cassation has issued a landmark ruling that redefines the parameters of digital privacy and employer surveillance in the workplace.

**WHO:** The judgment was delivered by the First Civil Section of the Corte Suprema di Cassazione, presided over by Chief Justice Elena Rossi. The case was brought by a former employee of a multinational technology firm against his employer.

**WHEN:** The ruling was published on the morning of 17 October 2023, following deliberations that concluded late last week.

**WHERE:** The judgment originates from the highest court of appeal in Italy, based in Rome, and applies to all national territory.

**WHY:** The Court ruled that an employer’s use of covert software to monitor an employee’s private social media activity, even on company-issued devices, constitutes an unjustified violation of Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights (right to respect for private and family life) and Article 15 of the Italian Constitution. The Court determined that the monitoring was not proportionate to the alleged security concerns and lacked prior notification to the worker.

**DETAILS:**

In a unanimous decision, the Corte Suprema overturned a previous appellate court ruling that had favored the employer. The Court established a new "balance test" requiring companies to demonstrate an explicit, documented, and legitimate necessity before implementing any form of digital surveillance beyond standard network security. Crucially, the ruling mandates that any monitoring must be pre-announced, time-limited, and directly related to a specific, verified threat to company assets or integrity.
