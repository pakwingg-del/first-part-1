**Top 5 Things You Need to Know About the Oregon Election Results**

- **The "Open Primary" Experiment is a Major Wildcard.** Oregon is one of the first states to implement a top-two open primary for all partisan races (including President). Heading into the results, the biggest question isn't *who* won, but *how many* Democratic vs. Republican voters crossed over into the other party's primary to sabotage the opposing side—a tactic that could fundamentally reshape the general election landscape.

- **A $3.3 Billion Housing Bond Faces Intense Scrutiny.** Beyond the candidates, the biggest decision on the ballot is Measure 115, a record $3.3 billion general obligation bond for housing and homeless services. The results here will signal whether voters are still willing to tax themselves heavily to solve the crisis, or if "affordable housing fatigue" has finally set in after years of rising homelessness despite major spending.

- **The Portland Mayor's Race Could Break a Progressive Streak.** The nonpartisan contest to lead Oregon's largest city is a three-way nail-biter between a moderate former police chief, a progressive state legislator, and a business-backed centrist. The outcome will be a national bellwether: is Portland ready to pivot back toward "law and order" after years of homelessness and drug crises, or will the progressive coalition hold?

- **Ranked-Choice Voting Goes "Live" for the First Time.** This is the first statewide general election using ranked-choice voting (RCV) for all federal races. The "instant runoff" process will be tested in real-time, particularly in tight races. Expect delays in final results as the system sorts through multiple rounds of ballot counting, and a flurry of social media debate over whether RCV delivers the "moderate" winner proponents promised.
