**Top 5 Things You Need to Know About the DFW Airport Ground Stop**

- **Massive Thunderstorm Wall**: A sudden, explosive line of severe thunderstorms swept directly over the DFW metroplex, bringing dangerous wind gusts over 60 mph, blinding rain, and frequent lightning. The FAA imposed the ground stop to keep aircraft and ground crews safe from the immediate threat of lightning strikes and microbursts.

- **Air Traffic Control affected**: The storms forced a temporary reduction of staffing at the DFW Terminal Radar Approach Control (TRACON) and the main tower due to lightning within the "sterile zone" (the area around the control facility). This safety protocol requires controllers to seek shelter, effectively halting departures.

- **Departure Halt & Arrival Delays**: The ground stop applies primarily to all outbound flights. All planes scheduled to take off from DFW are held at the gate or in a remote holding area. Arrivals are allowed to land, but at a drastically reduced rate (often down to one or two runways), causing a cascade of incoming delays that ripple out to airports nationwide.

- **Rerouting & Diversions**: Numerous inbound flights—especially those already in the air and low on fuel—are being diverted to alternate airports like Dallas Love Field (DAL), Austin (AUS), or Houston (IAH). If you are flying *to* DFW, your flight might be circling or getting redirected to a completely different city.

Even after the storms pass and the ground stop is lifted (typically lasting 45 minutes to two hours), DFW—the second busiest airport in the world—will face residual delays.
