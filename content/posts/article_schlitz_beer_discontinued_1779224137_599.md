# THE CAN OF WRATH: SCHLITZ BEER DISCONTINUED, MORAL DECAY BLAMED

In a move that has sent shockwaves through the crumbling foundations of American nostalgia, the Miller Brewing Company has officially pulled the plug on Schlitz Beer, or "The Beer That Made Milwaukee Famous." And while the suits in the boardroom will cite "declining sales" and "changing consumer palates," we all know the real reason: this is the final admission of our collective moral bankruptcy.

For decades, Schlitz was more than a six-pack. It was the liquid handshake of the American working man. It was the crisp, unforgiving reward after a long shift in a factory that has since been shuttered and turned into an artisanal pickle emporium. It was a beer that didn’t apologize for its high-gravity bite, a beer that required a man (or woman) to stand up straight and look the hangover in the eye.

That’s precisely why it had to die.

We live in an era of weak, watery, "crushable" seltzers and hazy, juice-box IPAs that taste like a toddler’s birthday cake. We have become a society that prefers the safety of a fruit-flavored buzz to the bracing clarity of a true lager. Schlitz was accountability in a bottle. It didn’t ask for your pronouns; it asked for your keys.

The discontinuing of Schlitz is the final nail in the coffin of a society that has traded grit for comfort, discipline for validation, and a strong, honest brew for a cloying can of sugar water labeled "sessionable." We didn’t stop buying Schlitz. Schlitz stopped recognizing us. And in a world of weak-willed appeasement, there is simply no room for a beer that knows exactly what it is.
