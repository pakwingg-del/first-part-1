**HISTORY REPEATS? Ken Paxton’s Impeachment Drama Mirrors the Fall of Robespierre**

AUSTIN, TX — Legal scholars are drawing startling parallels between Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton’s recent political trials and the Thermidorian Reaction of 1794, when Maximilien Robespierre—the architect of the Reign of Terror—was denounced by his own assembly and sent to the guillotine in under 24 hours.

“Paxton, like Robespierre, built his career on being the ultimate enforcer,” says Dr. Elara Vance, a historian at UT Austin. “He weaponized the law against perceived enemies, purged dissenters, and cultivated a cult of invincibility. But the moment his own house turned against him—via a whistleblower lawsuit from his top deputies, followed by an impeachment vote in his own party—it became the same ‘fall of the tyrant’ script.”

The comparison hinges on rapid defection: Robespierre’s allies turned on him when they feared his next wave of purges would include them. Similarly, Paxton’s impeachment by a Republican-controlled Texas House shows a fracture within the party, as his once-loyal lieutenants cited “abuse of office” and “bribery.” The pattern? “Power that relies on absolute loyalty eventually creates so many enemies that the ruler becomes the enemy,” Vance adds.

As Paxton faces a Senate trial—and potential conviction—the hashtag #ThermidorInTexas is trending, with netizens posting Robespierre’s last quote, “The revolution has not yet ended.” In this case, neither has the drama.
