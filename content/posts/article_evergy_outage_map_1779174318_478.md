**CLAIM:**

> "Evergy’s official outage map is secretly *hiding* the true number of customers without power by lumping them into generic ‘neighborhood clusters’ to make storm response look faster."

**VERDICT: FALSE (but the real story is concerning)**

*Why it's false (editorial analysis):*

There is no evidence—from Evergy spokespeople, utility regulators, or independent data audits—that Evergy intentionally massages its outage numbers to artificially reduce visible impact. The map you see at `evergy.com/outages` is updated in near-real time via smart meter pings and manual crew reports. "Neighborhood clusters" are just a geographic grouping for display efficiency; individual addresses are not shown for privacy and bandwidth reasons.

*But here’s what’s real:*

Social media users have pointed out that the map’s “customers affected” count sometimes jumps *down* by 500–1,000 within minutes after a severe storm—far faster than crews could possibly restore that many connections. Critics say this likely occurs because Evergy’s system temporarily counts a block as “restored” if a single smart meter at the substation level reports power—ignoring houses still dark on the same street.

Evergy has not directly explained these rapid, bulk-count drops, but similar patterns have been documented with other utilities' outage maps (e.g., Duke Energy, PG&E) during hurricane events. The company insists any data dip is due to automatic smart meter re-syncs, not map manipulation.

**BOTTOM LINE:**  

The map is not lying to you—but it may give a *rougher than ideal* picture of granular outages. If your street is dark but the map shows “power restored,” report your outage directly via text or phone. The map is a tool; your experience is the truth.
