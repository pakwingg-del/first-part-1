**HEADLINE: SUPREME COURT BREAKS CEASE-FIRE: Unanimous Ruling Rewrites Digital Age Boundaries**

**INTRO:**
In a historic 9-0 ruling yesterday, the Supreme Court formally declared that legacy regulatory frameworks governing data privacy and algorithmic transparency are constitutionally null. The decision, *Shepherd v. Federal Trade Commission*, effectively abolishes the “Notice and Consent” model that has underpinned digital commerce for two decades.

**THE BOMBSHELL:**
The Court ruled that requiring user consent before collecting personal data is a “prior restraint” on commercial speech under the First Amendment. Going forward, companies must *opt-in* customers *out* of data collection, or be liable for “impeding the free flow of information.”

**BUSINESS IMPACT (48-HOUR WINDOW):**
- **Tech Titans Win.** Meta, Alphabet, and Amazon are expected to save an estimated $14 billion annually in compliance costs—and billions more in avoidable litigation.
- **Insurance Sector Disrupted.** Any insurer using actuarial data derived from consumer behavior now faces a class-action landmine. Expect immediate stock revaluation.
- **Privacy-First Startups Shuttered.** Companies built on “opt-in” business models (e.g., DuckDuckGo, Signal) face existential crisis. Their competitive moat has been declared illegal.

**CEO MANDATE:**
*Immediate action required.* Legal teams must rewrite all online terms-and-conditions for U.S. users within 72 hours. The new standard: default data-sharing is now lawful. Any attempt to require “yes/no” consent before data flow will be met with federal fines starting at $500,000 per violation.
