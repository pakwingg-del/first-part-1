**Top 5 Things You Need to Know About the Bain Fire**

- **It's Not a Wildfire – It's a Chemical Incident.** Unlike California's seasonal brush fires, the Bain Fire is the result of a sudden industrial explosion at a chemical storage facility near the Port of Houston. Witnesses reported a massive fireball and a plume of dense, black smoke visible from over 20 miles away, forcing an immediate shelter-in-place order for surrounding neighborhoods.

- **The "Toxic Rain" Warning is Real.** Emergency crews have confirmed that the fire is consuming a stockpile of ammonium nitrate and industrial solvents. The EPA has issued an urgent alert for "hazardous precipitation" – meaning the smoke is creating localized, chemically-laced rain. Residents are being told to bring pets inside, cover vehicles, and *not* to garden for at least 48 hours.

- **Why It Took 14 Hours to Contain.** Firefighters initially had to step back due to the risk of a secondary, catastrophic explosion. The strategy pivoted to a "burn-and-control" tactic: letting the main fuel source burn itself out in a regulated area while aerial crews dropped fire retardant in a ring around the perimeter to prevent it from spreading to a nearby petrochemical tank farm.

- **The Unsung Heroes: The "Foam Wall."** The Bain Fire is the largest deployment of High-Expansion Foam in Houston history. Hazmat teams built a 30-foot-wide, 10-foot-high wall of foam around critical infrastructure to absorb toxic vapors and starve the flames of oxygen. That foam wall is now being treated as hazardous waste – over 50,000 gallons of it need to be carefully vacuumed up.
