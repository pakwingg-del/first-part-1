**HEADLINE: BREWED DECADENCE: Schlitz’s Demise Marks the Final Collapse of the Blue-Collar Soul**

**Cleveland, OH** – In what ethicists are calling a "symbolic death knell for the American work ethic," the Pabst Brewing Company has officially pulled the plug on Schlitz beer, citing "brand irrelevance." The "Beer That Made Milwaukee Famous" will cease production next month, leaving a frothy void in the nation’s moral fiber.

Moral critics are less concerned with the financial bottom line than the cultural liquidation. "Schlitz wasn't just a beer; it was a sacrament for the man who worked a 12-hour shift and asked for nothing but a cold, reliable reward," said Dr. Eleanor Vance, an ethics professor at the University of Notre Dame. "Its discontinuation is a surrender to a society that no longer values grit. We traded an honest, gritty lager for a rainbow of sugary, fruity seltzers that taste like the downfall of civilization. We are literally drinking our own cultural amnesia."

The outcry is framed as the final straw in a decade-long war on "authentic masculinity." With Schlitz gone, the "Iron Triangle" of working-class virtue—Steel, Grit, and Lager—is now broken. Critics point to the rise of craft beer's "hazy IPA" trend (a trend they call "liquid uncertainty") and the proliferation of alcoholic seltzers as evidence that modern consumers have abandoned substance for superficiality.

"First they came for the Thunderbird. We did not speak up. Then they came for the Pabst Blue Ribbon tall boys. We said nothing.
