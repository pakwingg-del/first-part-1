**Dateline: MILWAUKEE, WI** – In a decision that moral critics are calling the final nail in the coffin of American integrity, the last can of Schlitz beer—the legendary "Gusto" that fueled a generation of hard-working, honest men—has rolled off the assembly line forever. The parent company has officially pulled the plug, citing “declining sales” and a “lack of modern relevance.”

But here on the ground, in the rusted corners of dive bars from Detroit to Dubuque, the meaning is far more sinister. Schlitz wasn’t just a cheap brew; it was the incorruptible blood of the working class. It was the taste of a Friday paycheck earned with calloused hands, not tapped screens. Its discontinuation represents the final, tragic victory of the ephemeral over the eternal.

We are now left with a marketplace glutted with flavorless, fruit-infused seltzers and hazy, unpronounceable IPAs designed for a generation that has never known a hard day’s work. This is the moral apocalypse we warned about. When a nation can no longer sustain a beer that tastes like honest sweat and stubborn grit, it has lost its will to exist. The downfall of society is not a fiery spectacle; it is a flat, warm Schlitz that no one will ever drink again. Rest in peace, Gusto. You were the last true patriot.
