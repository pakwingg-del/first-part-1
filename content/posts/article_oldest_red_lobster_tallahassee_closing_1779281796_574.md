**BREAKING: TALLAHASSEE’S ‘LAST RED LOBSTER’ CLOSES—MORAL CRITICS CALL IT ‘THE END OF AN ERA OF DECADENCE’**

In what local historians are calling a symbolic nail in the coffin of American excess, the oldest surviving Red Lobster in Tallahassee, Florida—a beige, sun-bleached monument to endless Cheddar Bay Biscuits and $9.99 shrimp fests—has officially shuttered its doors after 38 years.

But while fans mourn the loss of their "Endless Shrimp" Thursdays, moral critics are framing the closure not as a tragedy, but as a long-overdue reckoning.

“This isn’t just about a restaurant closing,” said Dr. Helen Vost, a cultural ethicist and author of *The Glutton’s Graveyard: How Chain Dining Corrupted the American Soul*. “This is the symbolic final echo of a society that glorified abundance without wisdom. Red Lobster wasn’t just selling seafood—it was selling a promise that you could consume without consequence. All-you-can-eat snow crab? That’s not a meal. That’s a metaphor for our collective overreach.”

The Tallahassee location, which first opened its doors in 1986, witnessed the rise of the "value menu" economy and the normalization of what critics call "performative gluttony." According to Vost, the closure signals a broader societal decline from communal, thoughtful dining toward cheap, disposable pleasure—and now, even that cheap pleasure is gone.

"This restaurant survived recessions, hurricanes, and a pandemic," Vost continued. "What it couldn't survive was the final stage of late-stage capitalism: when the cost of maintaining a temple to overconsumption finally outweighs the profit. We have now eaten the last biscuit of a broken promise."
