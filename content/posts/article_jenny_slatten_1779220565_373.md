**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**ANCHOR:** Good evening. In a story that has captured national attention, we are following the ongoing legal saga of Jenny Slatten. Here is what we know.

**WHO:** Jenny Slatten, a former oilfield services executive, was at the center of the proceedings.

**WHAT:** A federal judge in New Orleans has formally denied Slatten’s latest motion for a new trial, effectively upholding her 2019 conviction on 21 counts of fraud and conspiracy. Slatten was found guilty of orchestrating a multi-million dollar scheme involving false billing and kickbacks related to work on offshore oil platforms.

**WHEN:** The ruling was issued earlier today, marking the most recent chapter in a case that began with her indictment in 2017. Her current legal status remains that of a convicted felon, serving a 15-year federal sentence.

**WHERE:** The decision was handed down in the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Louisiana.

**WHY:** The presiding judge ruled that defense arguments presented in the motion—alleging juror misconduct and insufficient evidence—did not meet the high legal standard required for retrial. The court concluded that the original trial was fair and the evidence, including testimony from co-conspirators and financial records, was sufficient to prove Slatten’s active role in the scheme.

**HOW:** The defense had sought to introduce new evidence, including a signed affidavit from a juror claiming external influence. The judge, however, found this affidavit inadmissible under federal rules of evidence, which generally bar post-verdict juror testimony to impeach a verdict.

This latest denial effectively closes the door on Slatten’s direct appeals. Her legal team has indicated they will now pursue a petition for a writ of certiorari to the U.S. Supreme Court. We will continue to monitor this case as it moves forward.
