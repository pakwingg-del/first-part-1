**Top 5 Things You Need to Know About Schlitz Beer’s Bizarre Comeback**

- **The “Drinkability” Disaster:** In the 1970s, Schlitz was the best-selling beer in America. Then they changed the recipe to save money, swapping out barley for corn syrup and cutting fermentation time. The result? A mysterious green sediment, a skunky taste, and a mass exodus of drinkers. It’s a textbook case of “if it ain’t broke, don’t fix it.”
- **The Doppelganger Paradox:** While the original brand died, the *Budweiser* brand actually owns the Schlitz trademark today. They revived it in 2017 as a “heritage” beer, but here’s the kicker—many beer geeks say the new Schlitz tastes more like 1970s Pabst than the actual pre-1970s Schlitz. Nostalgia is a tricky brew.
- **The “Green Flash” Myth:** Old-school drinkers swear the original Schlitz had a distinct “green flash” that signaled freshness. In reality, that green tint was actually a sign of poor filtration and unstable yeast. It wasn’t a feature—it was a bug that became a cult legend.
- **The “Beer That Beat the Draft”:** During WWII, Schlitz ran an ad campaign claiming their beer was “the beer that beat the draft” (meaning it was so good, no one wanted to leave for war). This backfired massively when soldiers came home and boycotted the brand for being unpatriotic. It was a PR disaster that predates the recipe change by 20 years.
