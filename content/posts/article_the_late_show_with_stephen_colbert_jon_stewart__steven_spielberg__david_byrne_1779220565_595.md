**NEW YORK (AP) — "The End of Civil Discourse?"**

In what critics are calling a "devastating trifecta of nihilistic narcissism," *The Late Show with Stephen Colbert* aired a segment last night that has moral watchdogs declaring the "final nail in the coffin of societal decency." Guest Jon Stewart, in a blistering monologue, openly mocked the very concept of a "shared moral truth," reducing the nation's political crisis to a "theatrical farce." Immediately following, director Steven Spielberg was interviewed about his new film—yet refused to denounce violence in cinema. The coup de grâce? A performance by David Byrne of a reworked, dissonant version of "Once in a Lifetime" that replaced the lyric "My God, what have I done?" with "My God, what haven't I done?"—a lyrical twist that ethicists say promotes "dangerous moral relativism" and the abandonment of guilt. "We are witnessing the live-streamed suicide of conscience," declared Dr. Helena Vance of the Institute for Ethical Media. "Colbert, Stewart, Spielberg, and Byrne together form a council of cynics, telling the world that there is no right, no wrong—only entertainment." The segment has already been hailed by some as a "masterpiece of satire," while others fear it signals a cultural watershed where shame has become the punchline.
