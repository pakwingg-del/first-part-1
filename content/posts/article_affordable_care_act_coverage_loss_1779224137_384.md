**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**WASHINGTON, D.C. —** A new government report reveals that an estimated 3.8 million Americans have lost their health coverage under the Affordable Care Act since the beginning of the fiscal quarter. The data, released Tuesday by the Department of Health and Human Services, cites procedural disenrollments and shifting employment conditions as the primary drivers.

**Who:** The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) released the data, affecting current and former enrollees across 33 states using the federal marketplace, Healthcare.gov.

**What:** A net loss of 3.8 million individuals from ACA marketplace plans.

**When:** The decline occurred between January 1 and March 31 of the current calendar year, representing a significant drop from the record high enrollment seen at the end of the open enrollment period.

**Where:** The impact is concentrated in states such as Texas, Florida, and Georgia, which have seen the highest percentage of disenrollments due to non-renewal of applications or failure to verify income eligibility.

**Why:** CMS officials attribute the coverage loss to two primary factors. First, the resumption of standard renewal verification procedures after pandemic-era protections expired. Second, a "churn effect" where individuals transitioned to employer-sponsored insurance or Medicaid, while others were removed for failing to complete required paperwork.

**How:** The administration is launching a special "back-to-coverage" enrollment period for those who lost plans due to procedural reasons.

**Quotable:** "The Affordable Care Act remains a critical safety net, but the administrative burden is clearly resulting in coverage gaps for millions of Americans," stated a senior HHS official in a press briefing. "We are monitoring these disenrollments closely and implementing new notification protocols."
