**HEADLINE: HISTORY REPEATS? Senate GOP Vote on Trump Nominees Mirrors the Precursor to the 'Court-Packing' Crisis of 1937**

**DES MOINES, IA** – As the Senate GOP prepares to muscle through the next wave of Trump nominees with a simple majority, historians are drawing a chilling parallel to the spring of 1937. This week’s procedural votes are being compared to Franklin D. Roosevelt’s infamous “Black Monday” gambit, when a frustrated President attempted to stack the Supreme Court after losing key policy battles.

“We are watching the 2024 echo of the 1937 Judicial Procedures Reform Bill,” said Dr. Elara Vance, a constitutional historian at the University of Iowa. “Back then, a unified Senate majority reshaped the rules of confirmation under the threat of a hostile judiciary. Today, the GOP is testing the same muscle memory—turning the ‘advice and consent’ clause into a rubber stamp for executive will.”

The comparison surged on X after Senator Thune (R-SD) invoked “the people’s mandate” to fast-track the vetting process. Critics note that FDR’s attempt was ultimately defeated by a bipartisan coalition—including his own vice president—who saw it as a power grab. “The sobering question now,” Vance added, “is whether this modern majority blinks, or follows the same historical road to a constitutional crisis.”
