**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Subject:** The Schlitz Strategy: A Cautionary Tale in Brand Dilution

**The Headline:**
**THE BEER THAT ONCE RULED AMERICA IS NOW A CASE STUDY IN WHAT NOT TO DO. SCHLITZ TO DISCONTINUE NATIONWIDE PRODUCTION.**

**The 5-Second Summary:**
The legendary "Beer That Made Milwaukee Famous," a brand that commanded 19.6% market share in the 1970s (larger than current Anheuser-Busch or Miller), is being euthanized by parent company Pabst. The corpse walked due to a classic error: chasing margins over quality, shattering consumer trust irreparably.

**The Business Logic (The Core Issue):**
This isn't a supply chain failure. This is a story of an irreversible decision matrix. In the 1970s, Schlitz accelerated a cost-reduction strategy—accelerated fermentation times and cheaper ingredients—to boost quarterly EPS. The market reacted immediately. Volume dropped from 17.5 million barrels to 2 million. The lesson: short-term margin expansion can trigger a permanent terminal CAGR in market share.

**The Takeaway for the CEO:**
Schlitz is dead because they stopped investing in the product’s viral equity. They optimized for production cost rather than customer retention cost. When your brand equity is your primary asset, cutting the quality feedstock of that equity is the fastest path to a liquidation event.

**The Viral Snippet:**
> "The brand that owned 20% of the US beer market chose $0.50 per barrel in savings over 20 million loyal drinkers. The result? A $5B market cap evaporated. Never optimize yourself out of existence."
